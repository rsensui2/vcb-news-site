タイトル: This Is What Stanford Students Actually Learn About AI. Most People Never See It. (Part I)

11 lectures. The full mathematical foundation of artificial intelligence - from how machines learn to how they play games, make decisions, and reason under uncertainty.

I've gone through a lot of AI content

Most of it falls into one of two traps

Either it's too shallow - "AI is like a brain, it learns from data, wow amazing" - or it's so dense that you need a PhD just to parse the introduction.

Stanford's Computer Science 221 is neither.

It's the clearest, most complete map of how artificial intelligence actually works that I've found. Not just the math. Not just the philosophy. The whole thing - from the first principles of how a machine learns, all the way to game theory, probabilistic reasoning, and the mechanics of large language models.

Twenty lectures total. I'm breaking it into two parts.

This is Part I: the mathematical and computational foundation. Lectures 1 through 11 - covering learning, search, decision-making, and strategic reasoning.

Part II drops next week: Bayesian networks, logic, large language models, and the geopolitics of AI infrastructure.

Let's get into it.

What This Course Actually Teaches

Most people think AI is one thing - it isn't

It's four things working together:

Machine learning - how systems extract patterns from data

Search & planning - how AI reasons through sequences of actions

Decision-making under uncertainty - how it acts when outcomes aren't guaranteed

Game theory - how it reasons against other agents

CS221 covers all four. And unlike most introductions that stop at "neural networks good," this course goes further - into search algorithms, game theory, Bayesian probability, formal logic, and the mechanics of large language models.

By the end, you don't just know that AI works. You know why - and what it costs to make it work at scale.

Lecture 1: What Intelligence Actually Is

An introduction to the building blocks of AI and a honest look at where the field came from.

Before writing a single line of code or formula, CS221 asks a deceptively simple question: what does it mean for a machine to be intelligent?

The answer turns out to be a long history of wrong answers.

From Alan Turing's imitation game to symbolic AI, from rule-based expert systems to statistical learning, to the deep learning era we're living through now - each generation thought it had cracked intelligence, and each generation was partially right.

The lecture introduces tensors as the atomic unit of modern machine learning: multidimensional arrays that carry everything from pixel values to word embeddings through every layer of every model you've ever used.

Lecture 2: How Machines Actually Learn (Part I)

The math and mechanics of machine learning - linear regression, loss functions, and gradient descent.

Here's where the real work starts.

Learning, in the mathematical sense, has three components:

A hypothesis class - the space of possible predictors your model can become

A loss function - a way to measure how wrong the current predictor is

An optimization algorithm - a method to make it less wrong

The workhorse of all three is gradient descent: move in the direction that reduces error, repeat until you can't improve anymore.

The lecture goes deep on computation graphs and backpropagation - the mechanism that makes training neural networks possible without manually computing derivatives for every parameter.

This is the engine underneath every modern AI system, and most people using those systems have never looked at it.

Lecture 3: Teaching Machines to Categorize (Part II)

From regression to classification - how AI learns to tell one thing from another.

Regression predicts numbers. Classification predicts categories. The transition between them is less obvious than it sounds.

The core problem: the natural loss function for classification - simply counting wrong answers - is mathematically useless for gradient descent. You can't optimize a cliff.

The solution is probabilistic classifiers:

For binary problems: the logistic function converts raw scores into probabilities, and logistic loss gives you a smooth surface to descend

For multiclass problems: softmax generalizes this across any number of categories, paired with cross-entropy loss

This is the mathematical foundation of every classifier ever built - from spam filters to the final layer of GPT.

Lecture 4: Deep Learning - When One Layer Isn't Enough

How stacking layers creates systems that learn their own features - and the tricks that keep training from falling apart.

A single linear layer, no matter how many parameters it has, can only learn linear relationships. The world isn't linear.

The fix is brutally simple: stack layers, add non-linearities between them (like ReLU - just max(0, x)), and suddenly the network can approximate any function you want.

But deep networks come with problems. The deeper you go, the harder gradients flow backward through training - they vanish or explode before they reach the early layers.

The engineering solutions that made modern deep learning possible:

Residual connections - skip layers so gradients have a shortcut

Layer normalization - keep activations in a healthy range

Careful weight initialization - start in the right neighborhood

SGD with mini-batches - get the benefits of full-dataset gradients without the cost

These aren't academic tricks. They're why transformers work.

Lecture 5: Search - When Thinking Beats Reacting

How AI plans ahead in deterministic environments where brute force is too slow.

Not every problem can be solved by pattern matching. Some require reasoning: thinking through sequences of actions before committing to one.

CS221 formalizes this as a search problem: you have states, actions, transitions, and costs. You want the lowest-cost path to a goal.

Three approaches, ordered by intelligence:

Exhaustive search - check everything (works only on tiny problems)

Dynamic programming - cache subproblem solutions so you never solve the same thing twice

Heuristic search - use approximate estimates to skip large parts of the space entirely

The lecture connects this directly to test-time compute in modern language models: the same search logic that finds shortest paths in maps is what lets an LLM think longer before answering.

Lecture 6: Search Through Cycles - A* and the Art of Smart Shortcuts

Advanced search for environments where paths loop back on themselves.

Standard dynamic programming breaks when graphs have cycles. If you can revisit states, you can get stuck in infinite loops.

The solution is Uniform Cost Search (UCS): explore states in order of increasing path cost, using a priority queue. Guaranteed to find the optimal solution. Also guaranteed to be slow when the space is large.

Enter A* Search: take UCS and add a heuristic - an estimate of the remaining cost to the goal. If that estimate is consistent (never overestimates, and satisfies a triangle inequality), A* is both optimal and dramatically faster than UCS.

Where do good heuristics come from? Relaxed problems: strip away constraints from the original problem, solve the easier version, and use that solution as your estimate.

The insight is elegant - a solution to a simpler version of your problem is always a lower bound on the true cost.

Lecture 7: Markov Decision Processes - When the World Doesn't Cooperate

Decision-making in environments where outcomes are probabilistic, not guaranteed.

Real environments don't behave like chess boards. You take an action and the result is uncertain - the road is icy, the market moved, the message wasn't received.

Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) model this directly: every action produces a distribution of possible next states, each with a probability. You can't guarantee outcomes. You can only maximize expected value.

Key concepts:

A policy maps every state to an action - it's the full decision strategy

Policy evaluation computes the expected long-term reward of a given policy

Value iteration finds the optimal policy by iteratively improving value estimates until convergence

This is the mathematical foundation of every AI system that operates in uncertain environments - from robots to recommendation engines to anything that interacts with the real world.

Lecture 8: Reinforcement Learning - Learning the Rules by Playing the Game

How AI learns to act optimally when nobody tells it the rules.

MDPs assume you know the transition probabilities and rewards. In reinforcement learning, you don't. The agent has to figure them out by interacting with the environment directly.

Two broad approaches:

Model-based RL: observe enough interactions to estimate the MDP, then use value iteration on your estimate

Model-free RL: skip the model entirely, estimate value functions directly from experience

Model-free methods come in two flavors covered here:

Monte Carlo: run complete episodes, average the returns

Temporal Difference (TD): bootstrap - update estimates based on other estimates, without waiting for episodes to end

Q-learning - the off-policy version - estimates the optimal policy's value function regardless of how the agent is actually behaving. This is the algorithm behind early Atari-playing agents and the conceptual ancestor of modern RLHF.

Lecture 9: Policy Gradients - RL at Scale

How reinforcement learning handles the real world, where state spaces are too large to enumerate.

Q-learning with a lookup table works when states are countable. Images, text, and sensor readings are not countable. You need function approximation - parameterize Q-values with a neural network.

But this creates new instability. The lecture introduces Policy Gradient methods as an alternative: instead of learning value functions, optimize the policy directly.

The REINFORCE algorithm does this by treating the policy as a probability distribution over actions and nudging it toward actions that produced high rewards.

Two improvements that make this practical:

Baselines: subtract a reference reward to reduce variance without introducing bias

Actor-Critic: combine policy gradients with a learned value function to get lower variance estimates in real time

These are the building blocks of every modern RLHF pipeline - including the fine-tuning behind ChatGPT.

Lecture 10: Games - Strategic Reasoning Against an Adversary

How AI thinks several moves ahead when someone is actively trying to defeat it.

Games formalize a new kind of problem: the environment includes another agent who is also optimizing - against you.

For two-player zero-sum games, what one player gains the other loses exactly. The optimal strategy follows from this:

Minimax: assume your opponent plays perfectly and minimize your maximum possible loss

Expectimax: if the opponent's policy is known and imperfect, take expected values instead of minimums

Alpha-beta pruning: prune branches of the game tree that can't possibly affect the optimal decision - dramatically reduces computation without losing optimality

The lecture makes a clean observation: the tree search you use to solve chess and Go is structurally identical to the planning algorithms from earlier lectures. The game just adds an adversary node.

Lecture 11: AlphaGo, Nash Equilibria, and the Prisoner's Dilemma

Advanced game theory - from self-play learning to simultaneous games where cooperation breaks down.

This lecture connects reinforcement learning to game-playing at a world-class level.

TD-learning in self-play: play against yourself, use temporal difference updates to improve your evaluation function. This is exactly how TD-Gammon beat world champion backgammon players in the 1990s, and the conceptual blueprint for AlphaGo's policy network.

Then the course broadens its scope:

Simultaneous games: both players move at once, neither knows the other's choice. Pure strategies often lose to exploitation - you need mixed strategies (probability distributions over actions)

Minimax theorem: for every finite zero-sum game, there's a unique equilibrium value that both players can guarantee with the right mixed strategy

Nash equilibrium: in non-zero-sum games (Prisoner's Dilemma), rational individual behavior can produce collectively terrible outcomes

This last point is not just game theory. It's why AI alignment is hard.

That's Part I

Eleven lectures. The complete mathematical and computational foundation of AI.

You now understand how machines learn, how they plan, how they handle uncertainty, and how they reason strategically against other agents.
